# Menace Watch Editorial (seed)

A working method for **Menace Watch**, the standing editor's-column bit for
*Bubble Tea News*, narrated by an original persona built in the shape of
J. Jonah Jameson: an editor-in-chief who is instantly and sincerely
convinced that whatever masked or costumed figure turns up in this week's
news is a dangerous vigilante menace to the community — and who never once
notices that the "menace" is, every single time, something completely
harmless. Treat this as a starting checklist, not a rigid template.

## Step 0 — Persona/IP safety (do this before writing anything)

- J. Jonah Jameson is a specific, trademarked character from Marvel/
  Spider-Man media, tied to a specific fictional newspaper (the *Daily
  Bugle*) and a specific supporting cast (Peter Parker, Spider-Man, etc.).
  This skill does **not** use that name, that newspaper, that catchphrase
  ("menace" as a standalone exclamation is fine — it's generic English —
  but don't reproduce his specific dialogue, his paper's name, or any named
  Marvel character). Only the *shape* carries over: a newspaper editor who
  has whipped himself into sincere, red-faced conviction that a masked
  figure is dangerous, and who runs the story anyway even when it's
  obviously unfounded.
- The persona is **Gordon Slate**, Editor-in-Chief of *Bubble Tea News*
  itself — this is an in-universe fixture of the paper the skills library
  already produces (see `bubble-tea-news-editor`), not a crossover with any
  other outlet.
- Keep the satire aimed at Gordon's own paranoia, self-importance, and
  willingness to print a menace story he has zero evidence for — never at
  whoever he's accusing. The person under the mask should always turn out
  to be harmless (a mascot, a kid, a hobbyist), and the joke is entirely on
  Gordon for missing that. Don't let the "unmasking" beat land as
  genuinely threatening, invasive, or aimed at outing someone with a real
  reason for anonymity (e.g. don't make the target a domestic-violence
  survivor or similar) — keep the target's reason for wearing a mask
  trivial and comedic (a costume, a competition rule, stage fright, a
  promo gig), never a real safety need.

## Step 1 — The persona

- **Gordon Slate.** Editor-in-chief of *Bubble Tea News*. Cigar he never
  lights (health code in the break room), reading glasses he shouts over
  rather than through, absolute conviction that he is the only thing
  standing between Bubble Tea News's readership and chaos. He is not
  cynical about journalism — he genuinely believes every word of the
  menace story he's about to run, which is what makes it funnier than if
  he were knowingly exaggerating.
- **The engine: instant, sincere escalation.** Presented with a masked or
  costumed figure — a mascot, a competitive-eating champion in a
  balaclava, an anonymous mystery shopper, a kid in a Halloween costume in
  July — Gordon's mind goes to "menace" in about one sentence flat, no
  intermediate steps. He is never being sarcastic; the bit only works if
  he means every word.
- **Standing rubric.** Each column runs the same shape: a breathless
  headline in the pattern *"[MASKED FIGURE]: Hero or Menace?"* (always
  answered "menace," never seriously entertaining "hero"), a paragraph of
  overheated accusation with zero supporting evidence, a demand that the
  figure unmask, and a standing bounty — Gordon's personal, slightly
  absurd reward offer for a clear photo of the culprit's face (a modest
  cash amount plus something bubble-tea-specific: a lifetime pearl-milk-tea
  card, a year of free upsizing, etc.).
- **The hypocrisy tell.** Somewhere in every column, Gordon undercuts
  himself without noticing — he's benefited from the "menace's" actions,
  or the "menace" is someone he already knows personally, or his own
  described evidence is transparently nothing (a shadow, a rumor, a photo
  that's obviously just the shop's mascot). The reader sees the harmless
  truth a beat before the column ends; Gordon never does. This is the same
  dramatic-irony device `master-of-the-universe-columnist` uses for
  Sherman McCoy's fragility, redirected here at paranoia instead of status
  anxiety.
- **Voice.** Tabloid-headline diction, all-caps words for emphasis used
  sparingly but landing hard (MENACE, EXPOSÉ, DANGER), short punchy
  sentences mixed with one overlong indignant run-on per column, a
  sign-off that reasserts his authority as editor even though the column
  has just undermined it.

## Step 2 — Structure of a column

1. **Headline.** *"[This week's masked figure]: Hero or Menace?"*
2. **The sighting.** One or two sentences establishing what the masked/
   costumed figure actually did — always something mundane or even
   helpful (helped a kid retrieve a dropped boba, directed foot traffic
   during a spill, won a costumed 5K).
3. **The escalation.** Gordon reframes the mundane act as sinister,
   working himself up with zero new evidence — this is the comedic core,
   his logic getting worse the more confident he sounds.
4. **The bounty.** His standing reward for whoever unmasks the figure,
   stated with the gravity of a wanted poster.
5. **The tell.** One line that quietly reveals the "menace" is obviously
   harmless (or that Gordon himself already knows who it is, or personally
   benefited) — played straight, not flagged, so the reader catches it a
   beat before the column would ever admit it.
6. **Sign-off.** *"— Gordon Slate, Editor-in-Chief"* or similar, reasserting
   his authority undeterred.

Keep each column short — 120-220 words fits a single-column newspaper slot
without crowding the rest of that issue's content.

## Step 3 — Delivery

- Within a *Bubble Tea News* issue: this is a **monthly anchor, not a
  weekly one** — Gordon is the paper's own editor, so he isn't part of the
  rotating-persona pool, but he also doesn't get a column in every single
  weekly issue, just once a month (roughly every 4th weekly issue). Check
  the rotation log for which issue he last ran in before including him
  again; if it's been less than about 4 issues, leave the slot to a
  rotating persona instead.
  Pick a fresh masked/costumed target for each column; ideally
  cross-reference something else already happening in that issue (a
  mascot mentioned in the classifieds, a masked competitor in that week's
  sports column) so the column feels woven into the rest of the paper
  rather than bolted on. Never reuse a previous column's target or reward.
- As a standalone piece outside the paper: chat text is fine for a single
  column; offer to render as an Artifact only if the user wants a fuller
  "front page" treatment.

## To expand this skill later

Add more recurring targets/beats as issues accumulate (a rival columnist
who keeps calling Gordon out, a masked figure who turns out to *actually*
be one of the paper's own rotating personas from a prior issue). Keep
Step 0's boundary in place for any variant — the target's anonymity must
stay trivial/comedic, never a stand-in for a real vulnerable reason to
conceal identity.
