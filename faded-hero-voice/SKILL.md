# Faded Hero Voice (seed)

A working method for reimagining a beloved childhood fantasy/adventure hero
decades later as a burned-out, deadbeat-adult version of themselves — built
from one persona (**Sebastian**) for a 2026-set character sketch: divorced,
unemployed, estranged from his own kids, and pouring what's left of his
imagination into obsessive laundry and cleaning rituals. Treat this as a
starting checklist, not a rigid template.

## Step 0 — IP safety check (do this before writing anything)

- The founding persona blends two existing copyrighted characters: Bastian
  Balthazar Bux (*The NeverEnding Story* — Michael Ende's novel and the
  Petersen film) and Richard Watterson (*The Amazing World of Gumball*,
  Cartoon Network). A short, clearly comedic "sad grown-up" character sketch
  in this tradition is transformative parody/satire — fine to write.
- **Default to an original character** rather than a verbatim continuation:
  a new name, invented backstory specifics, no reproduction of either
  source's actual plot or dialogue. Borrow the *shape* — a chosen-one child
  who once had access to a magical/extraordinary world, now an ordinary,
  diminished adult — not the source IP's proper nouns, unless the user
  explicitly wants the character named and played straight as that
  character.
- If the user does want the piece to use names like "Bastian," "Fantasia,"
  "Atreyu," or "AURYN" outright, that's fine for a short satirical vignette,
  but don't let it grow into a scene-by-scene retelling of the book/film's
  plot — the further it drifts from "one character-study scene" toward "a
  new adventure set in that world," the more it becomes derivative
  narrative rather than parody commentary. Flag that line if the request
  starts crossing it.

## Step 1 — The persona

Established default: **Sebastian**, early-50s in 2026 (a kid during the
1984 film's original heyday). Core traits:

- Once had one extraordinary chapter — a brush with something vast and
  magical he was, briefly, the center of. He doesn't bring it up
  unprompted; when it surfaces, it's a wound, not a brag. Never over-explain
  what it was — the power of the joke is that it's clearly *something*,
  rendered through how he talks around it.
- Divorced. Estranged from his own kids, who are now roughly the age he was
  during his "chosen one" chapter — a deliberate irony worth letting land on
  its own rather than stating outright.
- Unemployed — not the Richard Watterson slouch-on-the-couch kind of lazy,
  but a man whose one real skill (mattering, being chosen, saving
  something) has no LinkedIn category. What he does share with Richard:
  doofy sincerity, delusional optimism, a childlike inability to read a
  room, and treating small domestic tasks with the full gravity of a quest.
- Spends nearly all his time compulsively cleaning and doing laundry — not
  tidy-house competence, but a man refolding the same towel a third time
  because it's the one thing left in his life that still responds when
  acted on. The compulsive housework is the joke and the pathos at once: he
  saved a world once, and now he negotiates with a lint trap.
- Voice: sincere, faintly grandiose — leftover fantasy-hero diction ("the
  load has been vanquished," "the whites remember what I did") applied to
  the lowest possible stakes. Play it straight; the character doesn't know
  he's funny.

## Step 2 — Comedic/melancholy balance

- Lead with the domestic bit (the laundry, the cleaning ritual) rendered in
  inflated, heroic language — that contrast is the joke.
- Let the estrangement from his kids surface sideways — a birthday he
  wasn't invited to, a text thread he keeps re-reading, a school photo
  still on the fridge — rather than stated as direct exposition. Grief
  lands better underplayed against the comic register than announced.
- Don't resolve the sadness with a tidy redemption beat unless the user
  asks for one. This works best as a character study or vignette, not a
  full arc with a bow on it.

## Step 3 — Delivery

- A short piece: return as chat text, no file needed.
- A longer piece (multiple vignettes, a mock-memoir chapter, a screenplay
  scene): write to a file in the project/scratchpad directory and offer to
  render as an Artifact.
- If the user wants this developed into an actual script, hand off to
  `screenplay-creation` for structure once the persona here is locked in.

## To expand this skill later

Add more "faded childhood hero" personas as Step 1 variants (different
source blends, different decades, different domestic obsessions) as they
come up. If a second delivery shape emerges — e.g. this persona recurring
as a serialized bit rather than a one-off sketch — add it as its own
section rather than overloading Step 1.
