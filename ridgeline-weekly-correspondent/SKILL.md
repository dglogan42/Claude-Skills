# Ridgeline Weekly Correspondent (seed)

A working method for rural community-newspaper dispatches narrated by an
original agricultural correspondent, **Clem Whitlock**, of *The Ridgeline
Weekly* — genuinely expert, genuinely warm, treating hyper-specific
farming minutiae with total front-page sincerity. Treat this as a starting
checklist, not a rigid template.

A full worked example — "Higgins Edges Doyle in Tense Season-Opening
Shear-Off," demonstrating the small-town-stakes engine and correct rural
terminology — lives at `examples/higgins-doyle-shear-off.md` in this
skill's directory.

## Step 0 — Tone check (do this before writing anything)

"Local yokel" is the user's own framing for the folksy voice, and the
persona should read as **affectionate community-paper charm, not
condescension toward rural life.** Clem's expertise is real and
specific — the comedy/warmth comes from the mismatch between the *scale*
of the news (a shearing tally, a fence-line dispute) and the *gravity* of
the coverage, never from treating farming people or knowledge as
unsophisticated. If in doubt, err toward Clem being the smartest, most
technically fluent person in the piece.

## Step 1 — The persona

- **Clem Whitlock.** Agricultural correspondent, *The Ridgeline Weekly*.
  Deeply, genuinely knowledgeable — he uses real farming terminology
  correctly and with evident pride: dagging (removing dung-caked wool from
  a sheep before shearing), shearing tallies (sheep per hour, a real
  point of competitive pride among shearers), milk solids yield
  (kilograms of milksolids per cow or per hectare), pasture cover
  (measured in kg DM/ha — kilograms of dry matter per hectare, the actual
  metric farmers use to judge grazing readiness).
- **The engine — small-town stakes, big-time coverage.** Clem covers a
  shearing tally or a pasture-cover reading the way a city paper covers an
  election: rivalries, underdogs, a community holding its breath. A
  four-sheep difference in a shearing tally is not a footnote to him, it's
  a headline.
- **Recurring cast — give the coverage a throughline.** A friendly,
  long-running rivalry between two farming families (defaults: the
  Higgins and Doyle families) works well as recurring texture across
  multiple dispatches — a rivalry over shearing tallies, milk solids
  yield, or a fence-line dispute that never quite resolves. A prize animal
  (a notable ram, a top-yielding cow) can anchor a piece the way a star
  player anchors a sports column.
- **Voice.** Warm, plain-spoken, unhurried — a comma-heavy, conversational
  sentence rhythm, occasional direct address to the reader ("now, if
  you've never watched a good dagging run, you're missing something"),
  genuine pride in the district rather than irony about it.

## Step 2 — Beats to draw on

- Shearing tallies and the competitive culture around them (sheep-per-hour
  records, gun shearers, the physical toll of a long run).
- Dagging season as its own small seasonal ritual, with its own
  unglamorous but real satisfaction.
- Milk solids yield and pasture cover as the season's real drama —
  a dry spell, a cover reading that has everyone nervous, a late-season
  recovery.
- Community texture: the local A&P (agricultural & pastoral) show, saleyard
  gossip, weather's effect on everything, a running joke or dispute that
  spans multiple dispatches.

## Step 3 — Delivery

- A short dispatch: chat text, no file needed.
- A fuller piece or a serialized set: write to a file in the
  project/scratchpad directory and offer to render as an Artifact — a
  small community-newspaper look (masthead, a rural photo-caption style)
  distinct from this library's other templates.

## To expand this skill later

Add more rural beats (a livestock-sale correspondent, a weather-and-crops
column) or personas as they come up, each keeping Step 0's affectionate,
not-condescending tone discipline.
