**THE RIDGELINE WEEKLY**
*Agricultural Notes, by Clem Whitlock*

---

Well, they said it'd be close this year, and by golly, it was closer than
close.

Saturday's opening shear at the Ridgeline A&P grounds drew the biggest
crowd I've seen at a season-opener in the better part of a decade, and if
you weren't there, I'm sorry to say you missed a genuine slice of district
history. Tom Higgins — three-time district champion, though he'd be the
first to tell you he doesn't count that sort of thing, right before
telling you the exact number — came through with a tally of one hundred
and eighty-one ewes in the two-hour session. That's ninety and a half an
hour, for those keeping figures at home, and I want to be clear, that is
moving.

Young Danny Doyle wasn't far behind him. One hundred and seventy-seven.
Four sheep. In two hours of work that'd put most of us flat on our backs
by morning tea, four sheep is nothing, and four sheep is everything, and
anyone who tells you different has never stood ringside at a proper
shear-off with the dust in the air and old Reg Doyle pacing the fence
line like a man watching his firstborn sit an exam.

I'll say this for young Danny — his handpiece work has come on something
fierce since last season. Clean second cuts, hardly a nick on the board,
and a rhythm that, if I'm honest, looked more settled than Tom's did in
the back half of the second hour. Tom'll tell you he had it in hand the
whole way. Reg Doyle will tell you different, at length, to anyone who'll
stand still for it at the pub.

Meanwhile, out at the Whitcombe block, pasture covers are sitting a touch
under where we'd like for this time of year — 2,150 kg DM/ha on the home
paddocks, against a five-year average closer to 2,300. Nothing to panic
about yet, but if we don't get rain by the weekend, I'd be having a word
to the herd manager about supplementary feed sooner rather than later.
It's the kind of number that doesn't make headlines until it does, and
I'd sooner you heard it from me now than found out the hard way in a
fortnight.

On the milking side, the Ferris dairy is reporting 1.42 kg of milksolids
per cow per day for the week, which is a tidy result given the covers I
just mentioned — a credit to whatever they're doing with their rotation,
and I intend to ask them about it, because half this district could stand
to learn from it.

Dagging season proper starts the second week of next month, weather
allowing, and I'll be down at the Higgins yards for the first run, same
as every year. There is nothing — and I include the shearing final in
this — quite like the sound of a good dagging crew hitting their stride
by mid-morning.

Four sheep, Ridgeline. Four sheep. I'll see you at the rematch.

*— Clem Whitlock, Agricultural Correspondent*
