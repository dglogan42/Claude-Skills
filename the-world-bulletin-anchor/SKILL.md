# The World Bulletin Anchor (seed)

A working method for field-news dispatches from an original anchor,
**Baxter Kline**, of an invented broadcast, *The World Bulletin* — built in
the comedic tradition of a chaos-plagued news-flash bit (dogged, composed
reporter; hard news; increasingly absurd disaster) without using any
trademarked name, catchphrase, or branding. Treat this as a starting
checklist, not a rigid template.

A full worked example — an illustrative trade-summit dispatch demonstrating
the escalation structure and the catchphrase device — lives at
`examples/trade-summit-dispatch.md` in this skill's directory.

## Step 0a — Naming/trademark note (do this before writing anything)

- "Muppet" and its associated news-segment format/catchphrase are
  trademarked (The Muppets Studio/Disney). This skill deliberately does
  **not** use that branding, that exact catchphrase, or any Muppets
  characters — it borrows the comedic *shape* (composed reporter, hard
  news, escalating on-camera disaster) into a fully original anchor, show
  name, and catchphrase instead.
- If the user specifically wants the literal Muppets branding/characters
  for a short, clearly-labeled personal sketch, that's a different,
  narrower scope closer to `canon-fanfic-vignette`'s named-IP approach —
  flag the shift and confirm before writing anything that uses the actual
  trademarked names.

## Step 0b — Real-world accuracy, if covering real events

If a dispatch covers a real country, summit, election, or current event,
the chaos is comedic invention but the underlying facts should stay
honest.

- Default to **invented countries, summits, and officials** for illustrative
  pieces — this sidesteps the accuracy question entirely and is usually
  funnier anyway (an invented trade dispute can be as absurd as needed).
- If the user specifically wants a real event as the news hook, verify any
  real specifics via WebSearch before stating them, and keep the comedic
  chaos clearly separated from the factual hook so the piece can't be
  mistaken for actual reporting or misinformation about the real event.

## Step 1 — The persona

- **Baxter Kline.** Field anchor for *The World Bulletin*. Utterly
  sincere about the importance of the story he's covering, and utterly
  unable to get through one without incident. He is not incompetent — his
  actual reporting instincts and phrasing are sharp — he is simply
  standing in the single unluckiest spot on Earth, indefinitely.
- **Unshaken composure is the engine.** He never breaks character, never
  panics, never acknowledges the chaos as anything more than a minor
  continuity issue — this deadpan professionalism *through* disaster,
  not panic *about* it, is what makes the bit work. He narrates his own
  mishaps with the same even, authoritative delivery he'd use on a GDP
  figure.
- **The catchphrase device.** A fixed opening clause plus a variable
  disaster-status clause that changes every time, reporting his own
  condition with the same gravity as the headline: *"This has been The
  World Bulletin. I'm Baxter Kline, and—"* followed by whatever just
  happened to him — "—I am still on fire," "—that was, I believe, a
  goat," "—my chair has left the building," "—I regret several of my
  recent decisions." Vary the tag every time; the fixed opener is the
  anchor point, the tag is the punchline.
- **Escalation, not randomness.** Within a single dispatch, let the chaos
  build in clear steps rather than arriving all at once — a rattle, a
  small mishap, a bigger one, the finale — so the piece has a shape, the
  same escalation discipline `screenplay-creation`'s Step 5 toolkit uses
  for its own genre, applied here to slapstick instead of horror.

## Step 2 — Structure of a dispatch

1. **Cold open, played straight.** State the real assignment plainly and
   seriously — a summit, a trade dispute, an election result — grounding
   the piece as legitimate news framing before anything goes wrong.
2. **First wobble.** A small, almost-ignorable mishap (a mic feeds back, a
   gust takes his notes) that he talks straight through without
   acknowledging.
3. **Escalation.** One or two bigger complications, each interrupting the
   actual news content more than the last, each handled with the same
   flat professionalism.
4. **The disaster.** The dispatch's real set piece — the moment things go
   fully wrong — delivered in the same even tone as the headline, often
   funnier for the mismatch in register.
5. **Sign-off.** The catchphrase, tag reflecting whatever state he's
   actually in by that point.

## Step 3 — Delivery

- A short dispatch: chat text, no file needed.
- A longer or serialized set: write to a file in the project/scratchpad
  directory and offer to render as an Artifact — a news-chyron/broadcast
  look (lower-third nameplate, a "LIVE" tag, a ticker) suits this well and
  is a genuinely different visual object from this library's other
  templates.

## To expand this skill later

Add more anchors/formats as they come up (a co-anchor who never notices
anything is wrong, a weather-desk variant, a sports-desk crossover with
`lifeguards-eye-sportswriter`). Keep Step 0a's naming discipline in place
for any variant that riffs on another existing chaos-news format.
