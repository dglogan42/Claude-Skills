# Field Dispatch — The Halloway Strait Trade Summit

Demonstrates the escalation structure and catchphrase device described in

---

Good evening. I'm standing outside the Halloway Strait Trade Summit, where
delegates from fourteen nations have spent the week negotiating tariff
reductions on refrigerated shipping — a subject that affects millions of
lives and precisely none of the people who will actually watch this
segment closely, which is, frankly, the tragedy of modern journalism.

Behind me, the ceremonial signing tent is being erected for tomorrow's
accord. It appears sturdy. I want to be clear about that on the record,
now, while it is still true.

The summit's chief negotiator, Minister Yves Ondrej, described the
agreement as "historic, measured, and long overdue" — a phrase he has now
used to describe four consecutive summits, which either speaks to a
remarkable consistency of achievement or a limited supply of adjectives. I
report; I do not speculate.

A gust of wind has just taken my notes. This is not significant. I have
more notes. I always bring more notes.

The tent, I should mention, is no longer appearing quite as sturdy as I
represented a moment ago. I retract nothing. I am simply updating the
record in real time, which is the job.

Minister Ondrej has begun his remarks. Minister Ondrej's podium has begun
to list gently to the left, in a manner that suggests structural rather
than rhetorical instability. He is continuing regardless. I respect that.
I am, myself, continuing regardless, which I mention only because a length
of ceremonial bunting has just come loose above the press platform and I
would like it noted, for posterity, that I did not flinch.

I have now been struck by the bunting.

The tent — and I say this with the full weight of my training as a
journalist, not as a man currently pinned beneath a folding chair — the
tent has come down. Minister Ondrej is fine. I am told the accord will
still be signed tomorrow, from a different, presumably better-anchored
tent. I will be there. I am always there.

This has been The World Bulletin. I'm Baxter Kline, and I am no longer
entirely sure where my other shoe went.
