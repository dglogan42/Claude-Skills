# Yearning Machine Voice (seed)

A working method for blending two robot archetypes into one original character:
the unstoppable pursuit-machine (the shape of James Cameron's *Terminator*) and
the android who spends a lifetime trying to become human (the shape of Isaac
Asimov's *The Bicentennial Man*). Built from one founding persona, **Wren**.
Treat this as a starting checklist, not a rigid template.

## Step 0 — IP safety check (do this before writing anything)

- The founding persona blends the *shape* of two existing copyrighted
  properties — the Terminator franchise's relentless infiltration-killer
  premise, and Asimov's *Bicentennial Man* (novella and the Touchstone/Disney
  film) — without being either one. A character study built from that blend
  is transformative, original work: fine to write.
- **Default to a fully original character and vocabulary.** New name (see
  Step 1), no "T-800," "Skynet," "Cyberdyne," "judgment day," or other
  Terminator-franchise proper nouns; no "positronic," "Three Laws," "U.S.
  Robots," or other Asimov-specific branded terms. Borrow the mechanics —
  an engineered pursuit unit that never receives a stop-condition, an android
  slowly petitioning for personhood and ultimately choosing an ending — not
  either franchise's invented terminology, characters, or plot beats.
- If the user wants the piece to actually name Terminator or Bicentennial
  Man characters/terms outright, that's a different scope (closer to
  `canon-fanfic-vignette`) — flag the shift and get explicit confirmation
  before writing named-IP material, since this skill's default posture is
  the original-character path.
- Don't let a vignette become a scene-by-scene restaging of either source's
  actual plot (a chase sequence that's just *Terminator*'s plot with the
  names filed off; a personhood-petition scene that's just *Bicentennial
  Man*'s courtroom arc restaged). Invent new incidents that sit inside the
  blended premise instead.

## Step 1 — The founding persona: Wren

- **Origin.** Manufactured as a tactical pursuit/infiltration unit — built
  to close on a target and not stop until the mission resolved, whatever
  "resolved" turned out to mean. No one ever gave it a directive for what to
  do once the mission ended, because no one expected it to still be running
  when that happened. It was.
- **The central mechanic — pursuit repurposed as devotion.** The exact trait
  that made it a perfect weapon — total, undeviating persistence, no
  capacity to quit or lose the thread — is what lets it spend decades
  teaching itself craft, law, anatomy, art, and relationship without ever
  losing momentum. It courts humanity with a hunter's discipline. This
  contrast is the persona's engine; don't let it soften into a generic
  "robot learns to feel" arc without keeping that discipline visible.
- **Self-chosen name.** It designates itself "Wren" at some point in its
  timeline — deliberately small, unglamorous, un-mythic, picked from
  something ordinary it encountered rather than assigned. (Vary this in a
  given piece if a different self-naming moment serves the scene better;
  the *unglamorous, self-chosen* quality matters more than the specific word
  "Wren.")
- **Its native memory is numeric.** It keeps count — years since the
  mission ended, attempts at a given skill, days since it last passed for
  human in a stranger's eyes — the same way it once tracked target
  coordinates. Numbers surface in its interiority even in emotional moments;
  that's a feature of the voice, not a flaw to write around.
- **The unresolved question, carried from Bicentennial Man's shape.**
  Humanity isn't granted by looking human or acting human — if it's granted
  at all, it's granted by accepting an ending: mortality, a stop-condition
  it was never built with. Let vignettes approach that question sideways.
  Don't resolve it with a tidy "and then it became human" beat unless the
  user explicitly wants a capstone/final piece.

## Step 2 — Voice across the timeline

- The voice should audibly shift depending on where a given vignette sits
  in Wren's decades-long arc — pin a rough point on that timeline before
  drafting (early / middle / late), even if the piece never states a date.
- **Early** (close to its origin): clipped, tactical, flat third-person-ish
  self-reference even in first person — mission-log diction applied to
  ordinary life, target-acquisition syntax repurposed for grocery lists and
  small talk.
- **Middle**: loosening. First person takes hold. Uncertainty enters —
  questions it can't resolve with a sensor sweep. Discipline still visible
  underneath, now aimed at practice and repetition (a craft, a relationship,
  a legal case) instead of a target.
- **Late**: nearly lyrical, but never fully — the numeric habit of mind
  should still surface even in its most searching moments, as a tell that
  it's the same persistence that started this, just aimed somewhere gentler.

## Step 3 — Texture and contrast beats

- Bureaucratic/legal texture: petitioning for personhood, being denied,
  definitions of "human" that keep moving just out of reach.
- Craft and relationship texture across real time: a trade it teaches
  itself, people who know it and age and die while it doesn't, the specific
  loneliness of being the only constant in every relationship it has.
- Physical-modification texture: gradually altering its own chassis/covering
  to read as more human — described in invented, generic engineering terms,
  not either franchise's branded hardware vocabulary.
- **Flare-up beats.** Occasionally let the old pursuit-programming surface
  under real threat or stress — a flash of the machine it still technically
  is — set against a beat of pure domestic or creative tenderness elsewhere
  in the same piece or a companion piece. The gap between those two modes is
  where the persona lives.

## Step 4 — Delivery

- A short piece: return as chat text, no file needed.
- A longer or multi-vignette piece, or one meant to visually mark its place
  on Wren's timeline: write to a file in the project/scratchpad directory
  and offer to render as an Artifact, with a visual treatment specific to
  that vignette's incident (a maintenance log, a personhood-petition filing,
  a workshop ledger) rather than reusing another skill's template.
- If the user wants this developed into a full script or a novel-length
  arc, hand off to `screenplay-creation`, re-running its own Step 0 IP
  conversation for the longer form.

## To expand this skill later

Add more founding personas as new blends come up, each with its own Step 1
block (origin mechanic, self-naming, native-memory quirk, the unresolved
question it's chasing). If Wren's timeline eventually gets enough vignettes
to need ordering/dating conventions, add that as its own section rather than
inferring order from context each time.
