# View From the Desk

*Illustrative example — invented firm, bond, and market activity; not real
companies, real trades, or real financial advice. Written to demonstrate
the grandiose self-mythologizing voice and the bravado-with-a-crack-in-it
device described in `../SKILL.md`.*

---

There is a particular silence that falls over a trading floor at 9:29, the
minute before the bell, that I have never once heard described correctly
by anyone who hasn't stood in it. It is not the silence of waiting. It is
the silence of an army the instant before it moves — three hundred men who
already know, to a man, that they are about to win, because winning is
simply what happens to men who deserve it, and I have never for one moment
doubted which category I belong to.

I took the Continental Grain twenty-years at 9:20 this morning, four
hundred million face, off a syndicate desk that didn't know what it was
holding. I want to be precise about this, because precision is the only
difference between a trader and a gambler, and I am not a gambler — I
moved size on that paper before Chip Ashworth over at Halloran Voss had
finished his second coffee, and by the time he understood what had
happened, I was already three points ahead and composing, in my head, the
version of this story I would tell at the Racquet Club on Thursday.

Chip will tell his own version. Chip always does. He went to Deerfield,
not Buckley, which I mention only because it explains a great deal about a
man's instincts under pressure, and says nothing whatsoever about mine.

By eleven the paper had moved against the broader tape — grain futures
wobbled on a crop report nobody at my level reads personally, that's what
analysts are for — and for one interval, I will admit, purely for the
record, that the position looked less like a conquest and more like a
length of rope I'd have to explain to Judy over dinner if it kept sliding.
I did not sell. A man who sells at the first wobble is not managing a
position, he is confessing to one, and I have never confessed to anything
I didn't first decide was true.

It came back by one o'clock, as I knew — as I *knew* — it would, and I am
aware of how that sounds, the certainty of it, and I am telling you
anyway, because the alternative is admitting that for forty minutes this
morning I stood at my desk in a twelve-hundred-dollar suit doing
arithmetic in my head like a man checking whether he'd locked the front
door, and that is not a sentence I intend to say out loud to anyone,
including, apparently, myself, just now, in print.

We closed the position at four. Eleven points. I will be at the house in
Southampton by nine tonight, and somewhere between the Midtown Tunnel and
the Long Island Expressway I will stop thinking about the forty minutes
and start thinking only about the eleven points, which is, I've found, the
actual job — not the trading itself, but the editing of the story
afterward into the only version worth telling.

Chip Ashworth will hear about the eleven points by Monday. He will not
hear about the forty minutes. Nobody ever does.

*This column reflects the voice of an invented persona and is not
investment advice, and neither Continental Grain Exports nor Pierce
Whitfield & Sachs are real entities.*
