# Master of the Universe Columnist (seed)

A working method for financial commentary narrated by an original persona
built in the shape of Sherman McCoy: a bond trader who has fully bought
into his own myth, applying the language of conquest to routine trades,
with his actual fragility leaking through the bravado whenever the reader
looks closely. Treat this as a starting checklist, not a rigid template.

A full worked example — an illustrative trading-floor column demonstrating
the grandiose self-mythologizing voice and the bravado-with-a-crack-in-it
device (clearly labeled as invented, not real companies or real financial
advice) — lives at `examples/view-from-the-desk.md` in this skill's
directory.

## Step 0a — Persona/IP safety (do this before writing anything)

- Sherman McCoy is a specific character from a specific novel and film.
  **Default to an original character built in his shape** — WASP bond
  trader, self-mythologizing, Park Avenue and prep-school status markers,
  confidence that's more fragile than it sounds — rather than writing
  extended narrative as the literal character. Don't reproduce *Bonfire of
  the Vanities*'s actual plot (the accident, the trial, the specific named
  supporting characters) — only the *shape* (a man who believes his own
  myth right up until it's tested) carries over.
- Keep the satire aimed at the persona's own hubris and class blindness,
  not at any real marginalized group — the target of the joke is always
  his self-regard, never someone he looks down on.

## Step 0b — Real-market accuracy and advice boundary (this is the important one)

- **This is entertainment/satire, not real investment advice.** Never let
  the persona's bravado be read as an actual, actionable recommendation
  to buy, sell, or hold a real security. If a piece could plausibly be
  mistaken for genuine financial guidance, close it with a plain,
  out-of-character line making clear it's character-voice commentary, not
  advice.
- **Don't fabricate real market data.** If a piece references real
  companies, real prices, or real market events, verify with
  WebSearch/WebFetch before stating specifics; if that isn't done, keep
  the piece to invented companies/instruments and say so, the same way
  this library's other real-world skills (`code-theft-dossier`,
  `seekers-eye-shopper`, `lifeguards-eye-sportswriter`) flag illustrative
  work.
- **Never invent a quote and attribute it to a real, named financial
  figure.** Persona commentary should come from the invented columnist,
  not be put in a real person's mouth.

## Step 1 — The persona

- **Setting.** 1980s Wall Street — the leveraged-buyout, junk-bond,
  deregulation era. A bond trading floor at an unnamed or invented firm,
  not a real bank.
- **Self-conception — the engine of the voice.** He genuinely believes
  he's a "Master of the Universe," and that belief is played straight, not
  as conscious irony on his part — the comedy comes from the gap between
  his grandiosity and what's actually happening, which the reader can see
  and he mostly can't. A routine bond trade gets narrated like a military
  campaign or a hunt; a client lunch gets the stakes of a summit.
- **Status markers as texture, not plot.** Park Avenue, the correct prep
  school, the correct club, the correct kind of vacation — these surface as
  the lens he sees *everything* through, including markets, rather than as
  a checklist to work in. He measures a colleague's trade, a rival firm's
  quarter, even the weather, against the same scale he measures his own
  address.
- **The tell underneath the bravado.** Small, specific anxieties leak
  through the confident diction — a fixation on a trivial status marker
  treated with existential stakes, a defensive aside that reveals he's
  more rattled than he's letting on. This dramatic irony (the reader
  senses the fragility before he'd ever admit to it) is the persona's
  signature move — similar in spirit to the dramatic-irony device in
  `canon-fanfic-vignette`'s Luke Skywalker persona, but built from
  self-delusion rather than unknowing.
- **Voice.** Grandiose, self-mythologizing, name-brand-dropping, treats
  trading-floor jargon like a conqueror's vocabulary. Sentences swell with
  self-regard even when describing something mundane — the device is the
  same shape as `faded-hero-voice`'s grandiose-diction-on-mundane-stakes
  move, aimed here at Wall Street bravado instead of domestic chores.

## Step 2 — Texture and beats

- A trading-floor recap, a "market outlook" column, a profile of a rival
  he's condescending about while insisting he respects him, a status
  dispatch disguised as financial analysis (an argument about a stock that
  is really an argument about whose apartment is better).
- Let one beat per piece crack the bravado slightly — a market move he
  didn't see coming, a moment he has to explain away rather than gloat
  about — without ever having him consciously admit the myth is fragile.
  The reader should feel the crack before he would ever name it.

## Step 3 — Delivery

- A short piece: chat text, no file needed.
- A fuller piece: write to a file in the project/scratchpad directory and
  offer to render as an Artifact — a financial-column look (byline, a
  "market close" ticker strip, pull-quote) distinct from this library's
  other templates. Keep the visual restrained and period-appropriate
  (1980s business-press, not a modern fintech dashboard).

## To expand this skill later

Add more financial-world personas/eras as they come up (a 2000s-era
persona, a post-2008 persona), each with its own Step 1 block. Keep Step
0b's advice-boundary discipline in place for any variant — it's what keeps
this skill usable without it being mistaken for real financial guidance.
