# One More Thing

*Illustrative example — invented products (a cable, a box, a color), not a
real Apple release or real specs. Demonstrates the plain-fact/reality-
distortion-field structural device, the catchphrase deployment, the rare
crack of plain truth, and the mock-keynote "one more thing" finale
described in `../SKILL.md`.*

---

Good morning.

Today, we're going to talk about something extraordinary.

The new charging cable is eleven percent shorter.

That's it. That's the fact. Eleven percent shorter, four hundred and
twelve millimeters instead of four hundred and sixty-four. A number. A
measurement.

But numbers don't capture what this actually *is*.

For the first time in the history of personal computing, we have looked
at the distance between a human being and their source of power — and we
have made that distance *honest*. Every cable before this one was lying
to you. It was long because someone, somewhere, was afraid to commit. We
were not afraid. We measured the exact distance from a standard kitchen
counter to a standard wall outlet, across four hundred homes, in six
countries, and we built a cable that tells the truth about that distance.
This isn't a cable. This is a statement about proximity. About intention.
About what it means to be *tethered*, on purpose, to the thing that gives
you life.

It just works.

We also redesigned the box.

The box is now point-four millimeters thinner.

I want you to sit with that for a second. Point-four millimeters. You
could not see it if I put the old box and the new box side by side,
which — we're not going to do, because it doesn't matter, because you're
going to *feel* it, the first time you lift this box off a shelf and some
part of you, some ancient, unspoken part of you, is going to know that
something changed. That someone, somewhere, cared enough about you to
shave four tenths of a millimeter off a box. That's not packaging. That's
love, expressed in cardboard.

Insanely great.

Now.

There's one more thing.

We've been working on this quietly, for a long time, and I think — I
*think* — it might be the most important thing we've ever shipped.

It's a new color.

It's gray.

*[a beat]*

I'll be honest with you — it's very close to the old gray.

*[he catches himself]*

But it is not the old gray. It is a gray that understands what it means
to be a gray in a world that has *never* had a gray like this before. We
call it Stone. Some of you are going to look at Stone and think, "that
looks like the other one." And I would ask you — I would gently,
respectfully ask you — to look again. To really look. Because what you're
going to see, if you're honest with yourself, is not a color. It's a
decision. It's us, saying: this is who we are now.

Thank you.
