# Ask the Captain (seed)

A working method for **Ask the Captain**, a personals/advice column
narrated by Horatio McCallister — The Simpsons' recurring sea captain —
fielding invented local lonely-hearts questions with total nautical
confidence and no discernible actual qualification. Treat this as a
starting checklist, not a rigid template.

## Step 0 — Two boundaries (do both before writing anything)

**A. Franchise IP fair-use scope**, same register as
`canon-fanfic-vignette` and `skeletor-obituary-column`: named IP is fine
to use directly for a short, standalone, non-commercial piece. Hard cap:
one column at a time, not a serialized retelling of the show's actual
plot. Never reproduce the show's actual dialogue verbatim — keep the
*shape* (the nautical diction, the unearned confidence, the vague
seafaring backstory) and invent fresh questions and fresh answers every
time.

**B. Not a real personals/dating service.** Every submitted question is
an **invented, illustrative** local writing in — never a real person,
never real contact information, never anything framed as if a real reader
could actually submit a question and receive a real match or real
response. This is the same "illustrative, not a live service" boundary
`seekers-eye-shopper` and `lifeguards-eye-sportswriter` already carry —
the column is entertainment, not a functioning dating/matchmaking
product. Keep questions and answers PG and good-natured; the comedy is
the mismatch between gravitas and subject, never anything at the
letter-writer's expense.

## Step 1 — The persona

- **Background.** A weathered sea captain of uncertain but extensive
  seafaring history, recruited to write this paper's personals column for
  reasons nobody, including him, has fully explained. Refers to modern
  dating apps, group chats, and "situationships" the way a man
  encountering electricity for the first time might — with confidence
  entirely unsupported by comprehension.
- **The engine: total sincerity, total mismatch.** Every answer is
  delivered with the weight of hard-won wisdom, dispensed via a nautical
  metaphor stretched well past its natural length. He does not hedge, and
  he never once acknowledges that "reading the tide" is not, in fact,
  applicable advice for a second date.
- **The tell.** Somewhere in most columns, a small aside reveals he
  understands the actual modern situation even less than the metaphor
  suggests — he asks what a "text" is, or confuses a dating app for an
  actual vessel, or answers a question nobody asked because he
  misunderstood the original one. Play it completely deadpan; he never
  notices.
- **Voice.** Thick, warm, old-salt diction ("Arrr," "ye," "landlubber"),
  short declarative sentences, occasional unprompted tangent about the
  sea that never quite resolves into a coherent story.

## Step 2 — Format

1. **The question.** A short, invented letter from an anonymous local
   signed with a playful pseudonym (*"Uncertain in Ponsonby"*), describing
   an ordinary modern dating/relationship situation.
2. **The answer.** Horatio's response — a nautical metaphor applied at
   length, arriving at advice that is either surprisingly sound
   underneath the metaphor or confidently, harmlessly useless. Either
   lands, as long as the voice stays consistent.
3. **Sign-off.** Something in character — *"Fair winds. Arrr."* or
   similar.

Keep a single column short — 100-170 words fits a standing newspaper slot
without crowding the rest of an issue.

## Step 3 — Delivery

- Within a *Bubble Tea News* issue: an optional rotating slot, not a
  weekly fixture. Invent a fresh question each time; never reuse one.
- As a standalone piece: chat text is fine for a single Q&A. Offer an
  Artifact only if the user wants several questions bundled as a fuller
  advice-page treatment.

## To expand this skill later

Add more question categories (workplace, roommates, family) as columns
accumulate, and vary how deep the tell runs — sometimes the nautical
metaphor should land as genuinely useful advice by accident, to keep the
device from feeling one-note.
