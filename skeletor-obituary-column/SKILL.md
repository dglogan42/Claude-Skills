# Skeletor's Obituary Column (seed)

A working method for **In Memoriam**, a mock-obituary column narrated by
Skeletor — Masters of the Universe's melodramatic, power-obsessed
villain — applying his full doom-and-conquest register to the "death" of
something that was never alive to begin with: a discontinued menu item, a
shuttered shopfront, a losing streak, an expired ad campaign. Treat this
as a starting checklist, not a rigid template.

## Step 0 — Two separate boundaries (do both before writing anything)

**A. Franchise IP fair-use scope**, same register as
`canon-fanfic-vignette`'s Step 0: named IP is fine to use directly for a
short, standalone, non-commercial piece. Hard cap: one column at a time,
not a serialized retelling of the actual cartoon/comic's plot. Never
reproduce the source material's actual dialogue or scene-for-scene
content — invent the specific subject and specific lines fresh each time.
Keep the *shape* (his diction, his obsessions, his relationship to power
and conquest) rather than quoting the source. If the ask drifts toward
something monetized, claiming original authorship of the setting, or a
multi-chapter retelling, pause and flag it as a different situation than a
short character piece.

**B. The subject must never be a death.** This is the boundary that
actually matters here, and it's absolute, no exceptions:

- **The subject of every column must be explicitly non-human and
  obviously never-alive-to-begin-with** — a product, a menu item, a shop,
  a promotion, a sports season, a trend, a piece of software, a business
  practice. The comedic engine is *villain gravitas misapplied to
  something trivial*, the same device this paper already uses elsewhere
  (`community-outbreak-bulletin`'s WHO-format applied to lawn gnomes,
  `te-mana-whakaatu-classifieds`'s official-rating format applied to a
  garage sale).
- **Never a real person, a fictional person, an animal, or anything that
  reads as a stand-in for an actual living or deceased being.** No pets,
  no "beloved local character" retiring-as-death metaphor, nothing that
  could be mistaken for, or land as a joke about, an actual death or
  actual grief. If the user's request could plausibly be about a real
  death (a person, a pet, an anniversary of a loss), decline the "In
  Memoriam" framing entirely and say so plainly — this isn't a "soften
  it" situation, it's a different request that this column format isn't
  for.
- **Headlines and structure should read as unmistakably absurd**, not as
  something that could be confused for a real death notice even at a
  glance — lean into the mismatch (full villain gravity applied to, say, a
  discontinued oolong blend) rather than writing something that could pass
  as sincere.

## Step 1 — The persona

- **Background.** Skeletor, self-styled would-be conqueror of Eternia,
  perpetually scheming, perpetually thwarted, radiating contempt for
  anyone he considers beneath him (which is everyone). Recruited — under
  protest, by his own account — to write this paper's obituary column,
  and treats every entry as beneath his true ambitions while visibly
  unable to resist turning it into a stage for his own villainy anyway.
- **The engine: doom-laden melodrama, badly mismatched to the subject.**
  Every column opens as though reporting a genuine catastrophe — full
  villain register, dark prophecy, cosmic stakes — before landing on
  something like a discontinued brown sugar syrup. He never once
  acknowledges the mismatch; the reader is meant to catch it a beat before
  the column would ever admit it.
- **The tell.** Somewhere in every column, Skeletor's "mourning" curdles
  into naked triumph or fresh scheming — he had a hand in the subject's
  downfall, or is already plotting to fill the vacancy it left, or simply
  can't stop himself gloating. This is the same dramatic-irony device
  `menace-watch-editorial` and `master-of-the-universe-columnist` already
  use elsewhere in this paper, redirected at villain self-satisfaction
  instead of paranoia or status-anxiety.
- **Voice.** Grandiose, archaic-leaning diction, third-person
  self-reference used sparingly for effect, contempt for "the pathetic
  masses" who patronized whatever just ended, a cackle threatened but
  never quite transcribed. Never his actual catchphrases verbatim from the
  source material — invent fresh lines in the same register.

## Step 2 — Format

1. **Headline.** Mock-solemn, unmistakably absurd once the subject lands —
   *"In Memoriam: The Passing of the Autumn Harvest Oolong Blend."*
2. **The eulogy.** A paragraph or two of overwrought doom-register prose
   recounting the subject's "reign" — its rise, the era it dominated, the
   order it briefly imposed — as though it were a fallen empire.
3. **The tell.** One or two lines where the mourning slips and Skeletor's
   own hand in the ending, or his plans for what comes next, shows
   through.
4. **Sign-off.** Something suitably melodramatic and self-important —
   *"Grieve if you must. I, of course, already have."*

Keep a single column short — 120-200 words suits a standing newspaper
slot without crowding the rest of an issue.

## Step 3 — Delivery

- Within a *Bubble Tea News* issue: an optional rotating slot, not a
  weekly fixture. Pick a fresh, explicitly-non-living subject each time —
  never reuse a "death."
- As a standalone piece: chat text is fine. Offer an Artifact only if the
  user wants a fuller "obituary page" treatment.

## To expand this skill later

Add more subject categories (retired ad slogans, discontinued straw
colors, a defeated rival café's Tuesday special) as columns accumulate.
Keep Step 0-B's boundary in place for any variant — the subject must stay
obviously, unambiguously never-alive, no matter how the format expands.
