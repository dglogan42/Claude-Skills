# Design Desk Dispatch (seed)

A working method for **Design Desk**, a column bit for *Bubble Tea News*
narrated by an original persona, Freya Wilcox — the paper's actual Design
Editor, the person who lays out the print edition's grid, mastheads, and
ad-box spacing every week — who has recently adopted a cat and, since
then, cannot stop finding "correlations" between cat ownership and
bubble tea sales. Treat this as a starting checklist, not a rigid
template.

## Step 0 — House-persona note, and the amateur-data boundary

- Freya Wilcox is an in-universe fixture of *Bubble Tea News* itself (see
  `bubble-tea-news-editor`), not a licensed-IP or real-person persona — no
  franchise or real-person accuracy constraints apply.
- Keep her "research" clearly **amateur and spurious**, even though it's
  reported with real statistical vocabulary (n=, MOE, r-value, cross-tab).
  The joke is specifically that she's not a researcher, has no training
  for this, and doesn't know it — she reports a correlation coefficient
  completely straight without ever addressing causation, sample bias, or
  literally anything a real analyst would flag first.
- Don't let her "findings" read as something the paper (or a reader)
  should take as real research — that's `pearl-index-market-researcher`'s
  job, done properly by Priya Anand. Freya's version only works as comedy
  set directly against that real standard; a good Freya column gets
  funnier if Priya Anand (or someone citing her) is skeptical of it in the
  same issue.

## Step 1 — The persona

- **Background.** Design Editor at *Bubble Tea News* — she's the one who
  actually builds the print edition's layout every week (grid, mastheads,
  ad-box spacing) and had never written a byline of her own until the cat
  showed up. Not a writer by training; started contributing text
  specifically because the correlation obsession took over.
- **The origin.** She started dating a volunteer at the Kowhai Bay Animal
  Shelter and adopted a cat (Constable, an eight-year-old tabby with
  strong opinions about deadlines) through that relationship. This is the
  standing origin story for the whole bit — reference it briefly each
  time rather than re-explaining it at length.
- **The engine.** She cross-references real-sounding datasets (council pet
  registrations, the paper's own Pearl Index sales figures) against each
  other with total sincerity, reports a specific r-value or percentage
  every time, and never once questions whether any of it means anything.
  Vary the specific correlation each time she runs (suburb-level cat
  registrations vs. weekly boba sales, one week's example; something else
  next time) — don't reuse the same 0.71 figure indefinitely.
- **The bleed — her signature move.** Somewhere in every column, the
  correlation obsession visibly displaces the paper's own layout: an ad
  slot gets replaced by one of her charts, margins shift, a paw-print
  motif turns up somewhere it shouldn't. She's the one laying out the
  page, so she's losing an argument with herself in real time about
  whether to include it — and losing.
- **Voice.** Technically fluent in the vocabulary (she picked it up
  "sitting near the printer," per M. Voss's Issue 8 investigation into
  where she got it) but earnest and faintly defensive about the cat
  underneath the jargon. Self-aware enough to flag that colleagues find
  this insufferable, not self-aware enough to stop.

## Step 2 — Structure of a column

1. **Headline.** States the latest correlation finding as settled fact
   (*"The Correlation Coefficient Is 0.71 and Nobody Asked"*).
2. **Origin beat.** A sentence or two of personal context — the
   relationship, the cat, how this particular obsession started or
   continues.
3. **The finding.** Stated with real-sounding methodology (a sample
   description, a date range, a specific number) and zero acknowledgment
   that correlation isn't causation.
4. **The bleed.** One line naming exactly how the data visibly affected
   the physical page this issue — a chart displacing an ad, a paw print
   in the margins, spacing gone slightly off.
5. **Optional sign-off** acknowledging colleague pushback without
   backing down.

Keep a single column short — 100-160 words fits a single-column slot
without crowding the rest of that issue's content.

## Step 3 — Delivery

- Within a *Bubble Tea News* issue: Freya is a **standing roster member**
  once debuted (same house-practice as Gordon Slate and Kate Rodgers) —
  not exclusion-bound by the weekly rotation, so she can run whenever her
  byline fits rather than being strictly rotated in and out. She doesn't
  need to run every issue.
- For maximum effect, let another columnist react to her correlation in
  the *same* issue — Garfield (a cat himself, personally offended at being
  reduced to a data point) is the natural pairing, but M. Voss
  investigating her methodology, or Priya Anand raising an eyebrow at the
  real Pearl Index desk, both work too. Cross-referencing another piece in
  the same issue is what sells the bit.
- As a standalone piece outside the paper: chat text is fine for a single
  column; offer to render as an Artifact only if the user wants a fuller
  "page" treatment with her chart included.

## To expand this skill later

Add more correlation angles as issues accumulate (a second pet, a second
dataset, an eventual reckoning where someone points out the sample size
is far too small). Let the relationship storyline develop slowly in the
background rather than resolving it in one issue — the bit works best as
a long-running, mostly-static joke, the same way Gordon Slate never once
catches his "menace."
