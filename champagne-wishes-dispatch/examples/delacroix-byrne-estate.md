# Champagne Wishes: The Estate of Vittorio Delacroix-Byrne

*Illustrative tribute dispatch — invented subject, not a real person or
real property. In the style of Robin Leach's Lifestyles of the Rich and
Famous. Demonstrates the straight-tribute format (Step 2, default variant)
and the humanizing-detail beat described in `../SKILL.md`.*

---

Darlings, hold onto your pearls, because today we are walking through
forty-two rooms of pure, unrepentant *excess*, and I would not have it any
other way!

We are in the hills above Lake Como, at the private estate of shipping
magnate Vittorio Delacroix-Byrne — a man who built an empire moving other
people's cargo across the world's oceans, and who has decided, quite
reasonably, that his own home should look like it belongs to Neptune
himself!

The entrance hall alone — *the entrance hall alone!* — features a marble
staircase imported piece by piece from a quarry in Carrara, the very same
quarry, I am told, that once supplied Michelangelo, and if that is not a
man putting his money exactly where his ambitions are, I simply do not
know what is!

Through the west wing, we find the library — seven thousand volumes, a
rolling ladder of hand-carved walnut, and, tucked in the corner, a globe
the size of a Volkswagen that, when you press a small brass button,
*lights up from within*. Vittorio tells me he bought it at auction on a
whim, outbidding, in his words, "two museums and a very determined
Belgian." Darling, I believe every word of it.

The pool — and my friends, this is a pool in the truest, most operatic
sense of the word — stretches the entire length of the property, edged in
hand-cut mosaic tile depicting the great sea voyages of history, because
when you have made your fortune on the water, you do not simply forget
it, you *commemorate* it, chlorinated and heated to a perfect eighty-four
degrees year-round.

And here — here is the detail that gets me every time, the one that turns
a house into a home, no matter how many zeroes are on the deed. In the
kitchen, alongside the eight-burner range and the walk-in wine cellar
holding some four thousand bottles, there is a single, slightly battered
wooden spoon, hanging on its own small hook, apart from all the rest. It
belonged to Vittorio's grandmother, who taught him to cook in a two-room
flat in Genoa before any of this — the marble, the mosaic, the
Belgian-outbid globe — existed even as a dream. He will not let the staff
touch it. Of everything in this extraordinary house, it is, he tells me,
the only thing he would run back inside a burning building to save.

That, my friends, is the secret nobody tells you about the rich and
famous — underneath the marble and the mosaic, they are, every last one
of them, still standing in someone's kitchen, watching someone they loved
teach them how to stir a pot.

Until next time, from Lake Como to wherever you're watching from tonight —
champagne wishes, caviar dreams, and a battered wooden spoon worth more
than all the rest of it combined.
