# The Silent Majority Report — On the Age of the Leak

*Speculative dispatch — Nixon reacting to a present-day phenomenon in the

They call it a "leak" now, as though it were weather, as though nobody's
hand were on the pipe. I have some history with leaks. I want to say that
plainly, at the outset, because I have never in my life run from a
subject simply because it costs me something to discuss it — that has
always been my problem, and, I would argue, my virtue, though the two are
more often the same thing than people care to admit.

A great deal has been written about what a man will do to stop a leak.
Less has been written about why a leak frightens a man in government more
than almost anything else can. It isn't the information, usually. Men in
this business are exhausted by information; it arrives all day, in
binders, in cables, in men waiting outside your office with a face that
means bad news before they've said a word. What frightens you about a
leak is that it means someone inside the room decided the room could not
be trusted with itself. That is not a security problem. That is a
marriage falling apart, and everyone still has to come to dinner.

I am told the modern press receives these things instantly now — a
document, a photograph, a private remark, delivered not to a reporter's
desk but to a phone, in the time it takes to walk from one room to
another. I confess I find this development almost admirable in its
cruelty. In my day a leak took effort. Somebody had to want it enough to
carry a folder out of a building. Now, apparently, wanting it is the easy
part, and I don't know that this makes for a wiser public, only a
faster-informed one, which is not always the same achievement.

I will say this, and I do not say it to excuse anything, only to observe
it honestly, which is more than I was given credit for in my own time: a
government that fears its own leaks more than it fears the conditions
that produce them has already lost the argument. You cannot plumber your
way out of a building that people want to leave information from. You can
only ask, seriously, why they wanted to.

That is not a popular thing for a man like me to say. It was not popular
when I half-understood it in 1971, and I understood it only half, which
is its own kind of confession. But I would rather be the man who
eventually saw the shape of the thing than the man who never looked at it
directly at all. History has been unkind to me on a great many fronts,
and on most of them I have made my peace with the unkindness, because it
was earned. On this one, I think, in the end, I saw further than I was
given credit for. I usually do, once the argument has moved on to
someone else's mistakes.

This has been The Silent Majority Report. Quietly watching, as ever.
Someone has to.
