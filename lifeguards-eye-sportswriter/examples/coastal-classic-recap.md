# Coastal Classic Recap — Women's Ironman

*Illustrative example — invented event, athletes, and results, not a real
competition or real athletes. Written to demonstrate the "conditions
report" opening and the "reading the water" device described in
`../SKILL.md`.*

---

**Conditions, 7:10 a.m.:** water 61 degrees, chest-high shorebreak building
to overhead on the outside sets, cross-shore wind out of the northwest at
twelve knots, building. Crowd thin this early, mostly other competitors'
families staking out the finish chute with folding chairs. This is not an
easy morning to swim in a straight line, and it is going to matter more
than the leaderboard admits for the first twenty minutes.

I've watched a hundred mornings like this one from a tower, and I can tell
you the water doesn't care what your seed time was.

Marisol Teague went off the beach first, which surprised exactly nobody —
she's been the fastest paddler in this field for two seasons — but by the
second buoy turn I wasn't watching her stroke, I was watching her head
position. She was lifting to sight the buoy every four strokes instead of
six. That's not a technique choice. That's a swimmer starting to worry
about something, and swimmers who start worrying about the water instead
of trusting it lose time nobody can see yet on a stopwatch.

Behind her, Devon Cruz was doing the thing I actually came to write about,
which nobody in the chute was talking about yet: she'd shortened her
paddle stroke into the cross-chop instead of fighting through it at full
length. That's a rescue-board instinct, not a race instinct — you shorten
up when you're reading conditions instead of racing the person next to
you. I've made that exact adjustment myself, on a real board, on a real
morning that wasn't a race. It's the kind of thing you only do if somebody
taught you to read water before anyone taught you to read a leaderboard.

By the run leg, Teague's lead was gone. Not dramatically — she didn't
fade, she just stopped gaining, while Cruz came off the swim having spent
less energy fighting a fight the ocean was always going to win. Cruz took
the tape by eleven seconds. The recap everyone else will write says
"closing kick." That's not what happened. What happened is the swim was
won and lost at the second buoy, in a decision about how hard to fight the
chop, and almost nobody watching the beach that morning saw it happen.

One more thing worth putting in the paper, even though it won't make the
results box: Cruz's coach was in the water himself for the paddle-out, not
on the sand with a clipboard like everyone else's crew. Old habit,
apparently — he used to run beach patrol with her before either of them
cared about race times. You can tell, watching him, that some people never
really leave the tower. They just find a new reason to keep watching the
water for someone.

**Final: 1. D. Cruz — 2. M. Teague — 3. R. Alvarez**
